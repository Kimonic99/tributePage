Fela Anikulapo Kuti

Producer, Multi-instrumentalist, Rebel and Bandleader

Here's a time line of Fela's life:

  • 1938 - Born in Abeokuta, Nigeria.
  • 1958 - Leaves Nigeria to study medicine at Trinity College, London. Instead goes ahead to enrol for music.
  • 1960 - Kuti married his first wife, Remilekun (Remi) Taylor, with whom he had three children (Femi, Yeni, and Sola).
  • 1961 - Formed his first band, Koola Lobitos.
  • 1963 - Returns to Nigeria and forms a new version of Koola Lobitos.
  • 1969 - Brought Koola Lobitos to Los Angeles to tour and record.
  • 1970 - Renames the band from Koola Lobitos to Nigeria 70 and starts making more politically conscious and explicit music.
  • 1977 - Kuti and Africa 70 released the album Zombie, which heavily criticized Nigerian soldiers, and used the zombie metaphor to describe the Nigerian military's methods. 1000 Nigerian soldiers attack kalakuta republic[his home] and destroy everything including infrastucture and master tapes, while inflicting injuries on both him and the people around him. His mother would suffer injuries that would later prove fatal.
  • 1978 - Moves to exile in ghana but returns the same year.
  • 1979 - Formed his own political party, MOP (Movement of the People), he nominated himself for president in Nigeria's first elections in decades, but his candidature was refused. and at the start of the new decade renamed his band Egypt 80.
  • 1984 - Sentenced to 10 years in prison on trumped up charges of currency smuggling, was freed the following year with the help of amnesty international.
  • 1986 - Kuti performed in Giants Stadium in New Jersey as part of Amnesty International's: A Conspiracy of Hope concert along with Bono, Carlos Santana, and the Neville Brothers.
  • 1989 - Kuti and Egypt 80 released the anti-apartheid album Beasts of No Nation that depicted U.S. President Ronald Reagan, UK Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher, and South African State President Pieter Willem Botha on its cover. The title of the composition evolved out of a statement by Botha: "This uprising [against the apartheid system] will bring out the beast in us."
  • 1997 - Died on August 3rd at the age of 57 from health complications.

A press release from the United Democratic Front of Nigeria on the occasion of Fela's death noted: "Those who knew you well were insistent that you could never compromise with the evil you had fought all your life. Even though made weak by time and fate, you remained strong in will and never abandoned your goal of a free, democratic, socialist Africa." This is as succinct a summation of Fela's political agenda as one is likely to find.

-- Artist Biography by John Dougan
